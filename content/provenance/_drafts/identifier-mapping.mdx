## Structural weaknesses of the current regime

[Identity is administrative, not cryptographic. Identifiers can be re-issued, unrevoked, or disputed without a canonical arbiter. Cross-registry reconciliation is manual and lossy. AI-generated works expose the gap between "we registered this" and "we can prove it was made this way."]

## Provenance tokens as a substrate

[Conceptual model: a content-addressed identifier derived from a hash of the canonical work, signed by the author's key at the moment of creation, timestamped externally (OpenTimestamps / blockchain anchor), and recorded in a distributed append-only log. Registered identifiers (ISRC, ISWC, etc.) become *aliases* for the token, not primary keys.]

## Mapping rules

### Token → ISRC

[Mapping semantics for sound recordings. Token fields: hash of master audio, signature over the hash, timestamp. ISRC becomes a registered alias. Re-issuance of the ISRC does not change the token.]

### Token → ISWC

[Mapping for compositions. Token binds to the notated work (score/lead sheet hash) + the list of contributing composers' keys. Split disputes resolve against signatures, not administrative record.]

### Token → UPC

[Mapping for release bundles. A UPC points to a set of tokens (one per track). The bundle itself has its own token.]

### Token → ISNI / IPI

[Mapping for parties. A person's token is their key. ISNI and IPI are registered aliases maintained by the respective authorities.]
